# The complete guide to the 2026 Biodesign Challenge

**The Biodesign Challenge (BDC) is the world's premier student competition at the intersection of biology, art, and design — and the 2026 edition introduces two new prizes that should excite any design student who codes.** The new **BDC Prize for Biodigital Excellence** explicitly rewards projects integrating biological and digital methods, while the **BDC Prize for DNA Futures** targets genomics-based innovation. For an MDes class already building tools with Cursor and AI, these new categories represent a rare competitive advantage: most BDC teams come from traditional design or biology backgrounds, not code-first programs. The 2026 Summit takes place **June 11–12** at Parsons School of Design and the Museum of Modern Art in New York City, with the gallery exhibition titled **"Convergent Life."**





## What the 2026 challenge actually requires

Unlike most design competitions, the BDC does not assign a single mandatory theme. Each registered classroom selects its own focus — sustainability, fashion, agriculture, architecture, biomaterials, medicine, water, ethics, or any application of biotechnology explored through art and design. Registration happens at the classroom level through an instructor, starting at **$1,750 per classroom**.



The competition unfolds across the spring semester with a three-round judging structure. In Round 1, instructors and BDC-matched expert consultants select one Finalist Team per classroom. In Round 2, finalist teams present at Parsons before judging panels that advance the Top 8 to Day 2. In Round 3, Top Teams present at MoMA to a full jury that selects the Overall Winner, Runner-Up, and Outstanding Prize recipients.

### Submission deliverables

Every finalist team must produce five core deliverables:



A **10-minute oral presentation** followed by 5 minutes of Q&A with judges

**Visual renderings and illustrations** capturing look, functionality, and use cases

A **physical model or prototype** (strongly encouraged)

A **1–5 minute project video** — a creative trailer, not a recorded presentation

**Presentation files** in PPT or Keynote format uploaded to a shared Google Drive folder



Teams unable to attend in person can submit a video for the **Outstanding Digital Submission Prize** only. An optional project website and social media campaign (tagging @biodesigned) are recommended but not required.

### How projects are scored

Judges evaluate four equally weighted dimensions on a 4-point scale:



**Narrative** measures communication quality — clarity of the oral presentation, responsiveness during Q&A, and how effectively visual renderings and physical models illustrate the vision. **Concept** evaluates the idea itself — originality, design effectiveness, and feasibility across scientific, cultural, and technological dimensions. **Context** examines the bigger picture — human impact (adoption, scalability, ethics), sustainability and regeneration (environmental impact, resource lifecycle, circular design), and risk management (safety, dual use, harm mitigation). **Reflection** assesses critical thinking — evidence of experimentation and iteration, self-awareness of biases and limitations, and engagement with complexity.



Teams earning **≥2.75/4** across all four dimensions receive Platinum recognition. Gold requires ≥2.75 on three dimensions, Silver on two, and Bronze on one.





## Three years of winners reveal what judges reward

Analyzing the 2023–2025 winners exposes clear patterns that should directly inform ideation strategy. The strongest projects share three traits: they address urgent real-world environmental problems, they demonstrate genuine scientific rigor (not just speculative fiction), and they communicate through compelling narrative.

### 2025: Chornozem — where drones meet soil science

The **Overall Winner**, *Chornozem* from Zurich University of the Arts (ZHdK), addressed agricultural soil contamination from Russia's invasion of Ukraine. The team developed a low-cost contamination detection method using a modified curcumin solution that visibly reacts to heavy metals, then repurposed military drones with computer vision to create contamination maps guiding targeted remediation. This project is especially instructive for a code-focused class — **it won the grand prize by integrating computer vision, drone technology, and biological detection**, proving that biodigital approaches can beat purely biological ones.



The Runner-Up, *BagasSeries* from the University of Pennsylvania, and other Top 8 finalists included *ECO-SKIN* (Parsons), *Living Records* (MICA), *SHORE thing* (CCA Architecture/UCSF/Maldives National University), *Kerra* (Stanford), and *SquidKid* (Northeastern). Notable was the **Outstanding Digital Submission** *Risou* from Keio University, Japan, which won the Terra Prize. The Outstanding Instructor went to Charlotte McCurdy at Stanford.

### 2024: Consume Our Consumption — speculative biology meets lab science

**Overall Winner** *Consume Our Consumption* from UT Austin isolated gut microbiome bacteria from superworms (*Zophobas morio*) capable of depolymerizing plastics, then developed a speculative medicinal product called "Plascetamol" — a probiotic enabling organisms to break down plastic waste internally. The project balanced rigorous wet-lab science with provocative speculative design. Runner-Up *Kan Beh* from Universidad del Istmo (Guatemala) brought **11 team members** and represented the growing international character of the competition.



Other 2024 winners included *Urban Vein* (NTU Singapore, Outstanding Social Critique), *Shell We Dance?* (CCA Architecture/UCSF, Outstanding Science), *pHen* (Moholy-Nagy University Budapest, Outstanding Art), and *Salt Minds* (College for Creative Studies, Outstanding Field Research).

### 2023: Mother Nacre — elegant biomimicry with plastics

**Overall Winner** *Mother Nacre* from MICA encased sentimental plastic objects in biomimetic nacre (mother of pearl), demonstrating that *Pestalotiopsis microspora* — an Amazonian fungus that consumes plastics — can be grown using waste plastic as its sole carbon source. The Runner-Up, *Orbweaver* from UT Austin, and the **Community Choice** winner *Strongum* from Universidad del Desarrollo (Chile) rounded out a strong year.

### Patterns that matter for a code-first class

**Recurring schools** dominate: MICA appeared in the Top 8 all three years, UT Austin won in 2024 and was runner-up in 2023, EASD Castelló won awards in both 2024 and 2025, and CCA Architecture has had finalists each year. **Plastic waste and environmental remediation** appear in winners across all three years. **International representation** grew from ~40 teams to 60+ schools across 20+ countries by 2025.



Most critically for this class: **the 2025 winner used computer vision and drones**, and the new 2026 **Biodigital Excellence prize** explicitly rewards digital-biological integration. Projects combining real biological processes with computational tools — sensing, visualization, data analysis, AI-driven optimization — have a structural advantage in the current judging landscape.





## Biodesign is not biomimicry — and the distinction matters

Biodesign refers specifically to the incorporation of living organisms as essential components in design. As William Myers defined it in his foundational MoMA publication *Biodesign: Nature + Science + Creativity* (2012): biodesign "leaps ahead of imitation and mimicry to integration and use, dissolving boundaries and synthesizing new hybrid living objects." Biomimicry *copies* nature's patterns and strategies. Biodesign *uses* living biological systems directly — bacteria that produce dyes, mycelium that grows into packaging, algae that generate energy within architectural facades.



Alexandra Daisy Ginsberg and Natsai Audrey Chieza frame it more broadly: "biodesign — which we broadly define here as the design of, with, or from biology." Two questions unify the field: **"Can biology do this better?"** and **"How will using biology change things for the good?"**

### The practitioners students should know

**Neri Oxman** pioneered "Material Ecology" at MIT's Mediated Matter Group, merging biology, computation, and design. Her *Silk Pavilion* (2013) used 6,500 silkworms alongside CNC-controlled robotic arms — silkworms completed a structure pre-laid by a robot, demonstrating true bio-digital co-fabrication. Her *Aguahoja* (2018–19) series featured water-based architectural structures made from computationally designed biopolymers (chitosan, cellulose, pectin) that are robotically fabricated and programmatically decompose in water.



**Alexandra Daisy Ginsberg** explores synthetic biology, AI, and conservation. Her *Pollinator Pathmaker* (2021) uses an algorithm to design gardens optimized for pollinators from the insects' perspective — a direct example of code serving biological goals. Her *The Substitute* reconstructs the movement of the extinct northern white rhino using machine learning. She won the 2023 S+T+ARTS Grand Prize.



**Suzanne Lee** grew fashion textiles from bacterial cellulose using kombucha fermentation, then founded **Biofabricate**, the premier industry platform bridging design and biotech. Her work catalyzed Stella McCartney's mycelium handbags and Balenciaga's EPHEA mycelium coat — proving biodesign can reach commercial scale.



**William Myers** codified the field through his MoMA publications and curated exhibitions seen by over 20,000 visitors. **David Baker** at the University of Washington won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AI-driven protein design tools (RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN) — software that designs entirely new proteins using the same diffusion model architecture behind DALL-E and Midjourney.





## Where coding and AI meet biology right now

For students already building with code, the biodesign landscape offers multiple entry points — from creative coding simulations to frontier AI protein design.

### Generative biological form through algorithms

The most established intersection uses algorithms to generate biologically inspired forms. **Reaction-diffusion systems** (Turing patterns), **L-systems** (plant growth grammars), **differential growth** simulations, **space colonization** algorithms (modeling vascular branching), and **diffusion-limited aggregation** all produce organic patterns implementable in p5.js, Processing, Three.js, Grasshopper, or Houdini. The GitHub repository **jasonwebb/morphogenesis-resources** () is the definitive catalog — covering growth algorithms, reaction-diffusion, space colonization, Voronoi tessellations, fractals, and agent-based swarm behavior, with links to code implementations across multiple platforms.

### AI-driven protein and molecular design

The most revolutionary current development applies diffusion models — the same architecture powering image generators — to design entirely new proteins never seen in nature. **AlphaFold** (DeepMind) predicts protein structures with atomic accuracy and has cataloged **200M+ structures**. **RFdiffusion** (Baker Lab) generates novel protein structures with ~15% experimental success rates for de novo designs. **ProteinMPNN** designs amino acid sequences 200× faster than previous tools. **Chroma** (Generate Biomedicines) can design proteins in arbitrary shapes, including letters of the alphabet. These tools represent biodesign at the molecular level — literally designing living molecular machines with code.

### Bio-digital co-fabrication and sensing

Projects like Oxman's Silk Pavilion — where CNC robots lay scaffolds and organisms complete the structure — point toward a growing category where computational design directs biological processes. The 2025 BDC winner *Chornozem* exemplified this by pairing biological contamination detection with drone-mounted computer vision. For students with coding skills, this is a wide-open opportunity: building software tools that **sense, visualize, map, or optimize** biological processes.

### Creative coding tools most relevant to biodesign

**Rhino + Grasshopper** remains the primary parametric/computational design platform, with plugins like Galapagos (evolutionary solver using genetic algorithms) and LunchBoxML (machine learning integration). **p5.js** and **Processing** are ideal for biological simulations and generative art. **TouchDesigner** enables real-time generative content and interactive installations. **Houdini** excels at complex biological simulations through node-based procedural generation. **Blender's Geometry Nodes** offer free open-source parametric design. All of these can interface with Python and AI APIs — a natural bridge for students already comfortable in Cursor.

## Strategic recommendations for a code-first class

The 2026 Biodesign Challenge is structurally favorable for students who code. The new **Biodigital Excellence prize** was created precisely for projects that integrate biological and digital methods — a category where most competing schools have limited expertise. The 2025 grand prize winner used computer vision and drone technology, validating computational approaches at the highest level.



Three strategic approaches maximize this class's competitive position. First, **build software tools that sense or visualize biological processes** — contamination mapping, growth monitoring, ecosystem modeling — where coding skill directly enables biological insight. Second, **use AI to accelerate materials discovery or biological simulation** — protein design with RFdiffusion, generative biomaterial optimization, or agent-based ecological modeling. Third, **create interactive biodesign experiences** — installations, apps, or platforms that let users engage with biological data or processes through digital interfaces, combining narrative power with technical sophistication.



The judging rubric weights Narrative and Reflection equally with Concept and Context. This means a technically brilliant project that communicates poorly will lose to a simpler project with a compelling story. Students should invest at least as much energy in their 10-minute presentation and 1–5 minute video as in their prototype. The strongest past winners — *Chornozem*, *Consume Our Consumption*, *Mother Nacre* — all paired scientific rigor with emotionally resonant narratives about real environmental problems.



The **keynote speaker** for the 2026 Summit is **Ray Nayler**, Hugo Award-winning author of *The Mountain in the Sea*, a novel exploring non-human intelligence — signaling that this year's BDC is oriented toward interspecies thinking and convergent biological-digital futures.
