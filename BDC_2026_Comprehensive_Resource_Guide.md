## 1. What Is the Biodesign Challenge?

The Biodesign Challenge (BDC) is the world's foremost international education competition at the intersection of **biotechnology, art, and design**. Founded by Daniel Grushkin, it connects high school and college students to a global network of leaders in academia, industry, and entrepreneurship who are defining the emerging field of biodesign.



The program operates across **60+ schools in 20+ countries** annually. Student teams work throughout the spring academic term developing projects that envision transformational applications of biotechnology, then present at the annual Summit in New York City.



**Core premise:** Society needs interdisciplinary thinkers to understand biotech's impact and to develop the next generation of solutions. The BDC explicitly bridges art, design, and biotechnology — fields traditionally kept separate.

## 3. Philosophy and Vision

### BDC's Core Values

**Educational Mission:** Teach future designers to "understand the debates surrounding biotech so when they are asked to design with it, they do so thoughtfully and ethically."



**Critical Engagement:** Encourage students to explore "biotech's entanglements within society — in the way it empowers people, contributes to structural inequities, and creates opportunities for change."



**Diversity of Perspectives:** "To thrive, we need a multiplicity of perspectives across disciplinary, professional, and cultural backgrounds."

### Three-Pronged Goals

**Community Building** — Collaboration among artists, designers, and biologists

**Talent Development** — Seeding the first generation of biodesigners

**Public Engagement** — Building meaningful public dialogue about biotech and its uses

### What This Means for Projects

The BDC is NOT simply a science fair. It values speculative design, critical thinking, artistic vision, and ethical reflection just as much as — if not more than — technical scientific rigor. Projects can be provocative, visionary, or critical. The strongest projects combine real science with a compelling narrative and thoughtful consideration of societal impact.





## 4. Judging Criteria (Full Rubric)

### Judging Structure

**Round 1:** Instructors and expert consultants select one Finalist Team per classroom

**Round 2:** Summit presentations scored by judging panels; top teams advance to Top 8

**Round 3:** Full jury determines Overall Winner and Runner-Up

### Presentation Format

**10 minutes** oral presentation

**5 minutes** live Q&A with judges

**15 minutes total** per team

### The Four Scoring Categories

Each section is scored on a scale where judges must average **above 2.75 out of 4** to indicate sufficiency.





### CATEGORY 1: NARRATIVE (Communication)

This is about HOW you tell the story of your project.



**What judges evaluate:**



**Oral Presentation (10 min):** How effectively the team explains the design, the needs it addresses, the science behind it, and how it could be adopted

**Live Dialogue (5 min):** Quality and depth of responses to judge questions

**Visual Renderings & Physical Models:** How well these illustrate the project's vision, functionality, and potential uses



**How to score well:**



Tell a clear, compelling story with a beginning, middle, and end

Use professional-quality visuals and a polished physical model

Practice answering tough questions — judges value teams that can think on their feet

Don't just describe WHAT you made; explain WHY it matters





### CATEGORY 2: CONCEPT (The Idea)

This is about WHAT your project is and how original/feasible it is.



**What judges evaluate:**



**Originality:** Is the idea innovative and fresh?

**Design:** How effectively does it respond to the theme?

**Feasibility (three dimensions):**

*Scientific Feasibility:* Current science validates the project

*Cultural Feasibility:* Integration with existing values; how it improves on current solutions

*Technological Feasibility:* Clear technology application and realistic implementation path



**How to score well:**



Research what's been done before — and push beyond it

Ground your speculative design in real science (cite papers, consult experts)

Acknowledge where the science is still emerging vs. established

Show you understand the cultural context of adoption





### CATEGORY 3: CONTEXT (The Bigger Picture)

This is about WHO your project affects and its broader implications.



**What judges evaluate:**



**Human Impact:**



What factors would motivate adoption and use?

Scalability across demographics and time

Ethical considerations: access, equity, use of living organisms, established precedents



**Sustainability and Regeneration:**



Environmental impact on living systems

Resource sourcing and lifecycle analysis using circular design principles



**Risk:**



Safety and dual-use concerns

Potential for harm and strategies for mitigation

Exploitation prevention



**How to score well:**



Think systemically — who benefits? Who might be harmed?

Consider environmental lifecycle from creation to disposal

Address risks honestly rather than ignoring them

Demonstrate awareness of equity and access issues





### CATEGORY 4: REFLECTION (Critical Thinking)

This is about your PROCESS and self-awareness.



**What judges evaluate:**



**Process:** Depth of experimentation and how iterations were incorporated

**Self-Appraisal:** Awareness of your project's positioning, what voices are still needed, and next steps

**Critical Perspectives:** Ability to identify problems and engage with complexities



**How to score well:**



Document your process thoroughly (failures are valuable!)

Be honest about limitations and what you'd do differently

Show you've engaged with perspectives beyond your own

Demonstrate genuine intellectual growth





### Medal System



A **majority of judges** (>50%) must score above 2.75 average per section for recognition.

## 8. Past Winning Projects (2023–2025)

### 2025 Overall Winner: "Chornozem" — Zurich University of the Arts

**Team:** Olivia Menezes, Yaroslava Shylyk, Kirill Kohl



**The Project:** Chornozem addresses the ecocide caused by the Russian invasion of Ukraine — specifically, agricultural soil contamination from heavy metals in munitions and fuel. The team developed a low-cost, field-ready contamination detection method by mixing a modified curcumin solution (which visibly reacts to heavy metals) into fertilizer. Using repurposed military drones and **computer vision**, contamination maps guide targeted remediation.



**Why it won:** Addressed a real, urgent global crisis. Combined accessible science (curcumin chemistry) with cutting-edge technology (drones + computer vision). Deeply contextual — connected to the lived experience of one team member. Made hidden ecological damage visible.



**Relevance to us:** This project integrated **computer vision** and **drone technology** — the judges clearly valued digital/computational tools applied to a biodesign problem.

### 2024 Overall Winner: "Consume Our Consumption" — UT Austin

**Team:** Anya Koehne, Annan Zuo



**The Project:** Harnessed the gut microbiome of Zophobas morio (superworms), which contains microbes capable of depolymerizing and degrading plastics. The team cultured frass samples to isolate bacteria that can consume plastic waste, then developed a speculative product called "Plascetamol" — a probiotic enabling both animals and humans to consume plastic, creating a closed loop of consumption.



**Why it won:** Provocative speculative design that drew a powerful conceptual parallel between "consuming" (eating) and "consumption" (plastic waste). Grounded in real science (superworm microbiome research is published), but pushed into a deliberately dystopian future vision. Strong concept + strong narrative.

### 2023 Overall Winner: "Mother Nacre" — MICA

**Team:** Maddie Olsen, Finn Yencken, Sarah Becker, Lily Xiao, Orin Noel, Riley Cox, Starling Wolfrum



**The Project:** Aimed to change people's relationship with plastic through the "Plastic Heirloom" — encasing sentimental plastic objects in biomimetic nacre (mother of pearl), a coating mollusks use for protection. Also explored Pestalotiopsis microspora, an Amazonian fungus capable of consuming plastics, using it to produce bio-based leather substitutes. The process creates valuable consumer materials while removing harmful plastics from the environment.



**Why it won:** Emotionally resonant concept (preserving sentimental objects). Combined two complementary biological approaches (biomimetic nacre + fungal plastic degradation). Strong material science grounding with clear consumer application. Beautiful narrative about transforming our relationship with waste.

## 9. Patterns of Winning Projects

### Common Traits of Winners

After analyzing three years of winning projects, these patterns emerge:



**1. Real Science, Speculative Application** Every winner grounds their project in published, verifiable science — but then pushes into a speculative, sometimes provocative future application. "Consume Our Consumption" is based on real superworm research but imagines a dystopian probiotic pill. "Chornozem" uses real curcumin chemistry but applies it in a novel field-detection context.



**2. Emotional Resonance** Winners don't just present functional solutions — they create emotional connections. "Mother Nacre" is about preserving sentimental objects. "Chornozem" is connected to the lived trauma of war. The judges respond to projects that make you *feel* something.



**3. Strong Visual Identity** Every winning project has polished, professional visual renderings and a cohesive aesthetic. This is a design competition — presentation quality matters enormously.



**4. Systemic Thinking** Winners address Context thoroughly: who benefits, who's harmed, environmental lifecycle, cultural adoption, and risk mitigation. They think beyond the object to the system.



**5. Honest Reflection** Winning teams are transparent about their process, failures, and limitations. Judges reward intellectual honesty over false confidence.



**6. Small Teams Can Win** "Consume Our Consumption" won with just 2 team members. "Chornozem" won with 3. You don't need a huge team — you need a focused, excellent one.



**7. International Perspective** Winners frequently come from outside the US (Zurich, Guatemala, Chile, Spain, Japan). Global context and diverse perspectives are valued.





## 10. Strategic Advice for Our Team

### Our Unique Advantage

As MDes students in Digital Art and AI Technology who work extensively with code (via Cursor), we have a distinctive competitive edge. Here's how to leverage it:

### Target Prizes

Given our skill set, we should specifically aim for:



**BDC Prize for Biodigital Excellence** — This literally rewards merging computation, data, interfaces, simulation, or digital fabrication with living systems

**Outstanding Digital Submission** — Accepts interactive websites, AR/VR, and even video games

**Outstanding Narrative** — Our design and visual storytelling skills are an asset

**Overall Winner** — Why not aim high?

### Project Direction Recommendations

**DO:**



Build an interactive digital experience (website, app, AR/VR, or game) that explores a biodesign concept

Use AI tools transparently as part of your design and research process — the symposium is literally about AI in biodesign education

Ground your project in real biotechnology — consult the BDC's expert consultants network

Create a compelling physical model alongside your digital work (required deliverable)

Develop a strong visual identity and narrative arc

Consider speculative/critical design approaches — imagining futures, not just solving today's problems

Address ethics, equity, and environmental impact head-on

Document your process including failures and pivots



**DON'T:**



Make a purely digital project with no biological grounding — the "bio" in biodesign is non-negotiable

Rely on AI-generated content without critical engagement — judges value original thinking

Ignore the physical model requirement

Skip the Context and Reflection categories — these are where many teams lose points

Present without extensive rehearsal — the live Q&A is where judges test your depth

### Process Recommendations

**Weeks 1–2:** Research biotechnology domains (medicine, materials, food, water, energy, fashion). Identify an area where computation/AI intersects meaningfully with biology.

**Weeks 3–4:** Develop concept and validate scientific feasibility. Use BDC's expert consultant network. Read existing literature.

**Weeks 5–8:** Build iteratively. Create visual renderings, develop digital components, prototype physical model. Document everything.

**Weeks 9–10:** Polish presentation, rehearse extensively, create project video and website.

**Final week:** Stress-test with mock Q&A sessions. Prepare for tough questions about feasibility, ethics, and risk.

### Presentation Tips

Tell a story, don't give a lecture

Open with the problem/provocation, not the solution

Show your process (including dead ends)

Make your visuals and physical model work together

During Q&A: pause before answering, be honest about unknowns, connect answers back to your framework

Dress professionally — you're presenting at MoMA
